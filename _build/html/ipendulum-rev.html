Galileo’s Interrupted Pendulum#

Ewout ter Haar
Instituto de Física, Universidade de São Paulo, C.P. 66318, 05315-970 São Paulo, SP, Brazil
ewout@if.usp.br

PACS: 01.40.gb, 01.50.Pa, 01.65.+g
Keywords: pendulum, “interrupted pendulum”, “laboratory experiment”, Galileo

INTRODUCTION#

On the third day of the Discorsi (Two New Sciences), Galileo discusses an experiment with an interrupted pendulum, in which the string is obstructed in mid-course by a pin. The bob, despite moving along two different arcs, still reaches the same height from which it was released. We transform the usual demonstration into an experiment by posing an intellectual puzzle and a motoric challenge for our students, the latter motivating the formulation of a solution of the former. Students can be made explicitly aware of the intertwining of inductive and deductive reasoning. We show how we used this classic experiment in the classroom and analyze the motion of the bob for the case that the string does not remain taut for the entire trajectory.
Galileo’s aim is to make plausible his postulate that “the speeds acquired by one and the same body moving down planes of different inclinations are equal when the heights of these planes are equal”1. Galileo’s experiment makes for a nice demonstration of conservation of mechanical energy. It is as if the bob “wants” to reach its original height, and if it can’t it will twist around the obstacle. As was observed2, students are intrigued by this simple demonstration.
To move from demonstration to experiment, we posed a simple challenge for our students: discover from what height we must release the bob for it to go just over the pin, so that the string entangles itself around it. This experiment is simple enough so that students themselves can set up a conceptual framework, make certain predictions as to the outcome and confront them with experiment. We shall see how the simplest hypothesis that our students come up with is refuted by their observations, forcing them to make a more sophisticated analysis.

THE EXPERIMENT#

We adapted our standard pendulums to include a movable 6 or 3 mm diameter brass pin acting as the obstacle for the string. If we let the lead bob go from a relatively small height, it will reach approximately the same height on the other side of the pendulum and return. If the height from which the mass is let go is large enough, the mass will go over the pin and the string will entangle itself around the pin (Figure 1).
We define hc and hp as respectively the critical height for which the string just entangles itself, and the height of the pin, both relative to the reference point where the mass is at its lowest point. Our challenge for our students was: from what height must we release the bob for it to go over the pin? In other words, determine experimentally and explain theoretically the relationship between the critical height hc and the position of the pin, hp.
We discussed two ways to proceed. One obvious way is just to do the experiment, determine hc for various hp’s, graphing the results (hc vs. hp), and then try to explain the results. This was the way most groups proceeded. An alternative, we explained, is to analyze the situation, make a prediction as to what hc would be for every hp, and check the prediction experimentally. A minority of groups saw almost immediately that the mass could not rise to the same height on the other side of the pendulum if the initial height was larger than 2hp, and proceeded to check the relation hc = 2hp. All groups grasped the task as it was laid out immediately, and proceeded to measure the height hc for which the weight landed on the pin (with a satisfying thunk) for a few hp’s. After only a few measurements, most groups quickly caught on to the factor 2, which now became the working hypothesis, as it was for Galileo1:

Now, gentlemen, you will observe with pleasure that the ball swings to the [same height] in the horizontal, and you would see the same thing happen if the obstacle were placed at some lower point […] the rise of the ball always terminating exactly on [the same height]. But when the nail is placed so low that the remainder of the thread below it will not reach [the same height] (which would happen if the nail were placed nearer [the lowest point] than to the [height from which it was released]) then the thread leaps over the nail and twists itself about it.

Our students are happy to have discovered this prediction, because they are able to work much faster now that they know what hc should be. Verifying the critical height is much more efficient than discovering it with many tries. With a little prodding, students can be made to articulate an “explanation” for their observations. It usually involves the above-mentioned “wanting to reach the same height” concept, which is a special form of the energy conservation principle for the case where the velocities at the two points under consideration are zero.
But unease set in for larger values of hc and hp, which can be measured with relatively larger precision. As can be seen in Figure 3, the results for our pendulums indicate values of the critical height systematically a little bit below the expected factor two. Although students are given to explain deviations from the expectations simply with a bland affirmation of “experimental error” or, in the case of mechanical experiments, “friction”, here they are aware that they cannot give such an easy explanation, as it would lead to values of hc above the expected ones.
The naïve expectation that the bob must be released from at least twice the height of the pin is based upon the erroneous assumption that the trajectory of the bob after it hits the pin is a semicircle, for all velocities. In fact, the bob can never be in a position directly above the pin with zero velocity, as was assumed in for example Ref. 2. A well-known problem standard textbooks3,4,5,6 , usually in the context of a “loop the loop” setup, asks to show that we must release the bob from at least 2.5hp for the string to remain taut.
For lower release heights there is an angle after which gravity cannot provide the necessary centripetal force, and the trajectory becomes a free-fall parabola. With the obvious idealizations (massless string, negligible pin diameter, no dissipation), the trajectory is as shown in Figure 2. For the trajectory to move through the origin we can show by simple but rather messy algebraic manipulations (see Appendix) that hc = hp = 1.866hp, which is 7% less than the original (and Galileo’s) prediction hc = 2hp.
Of course, energy losses in flight or when the string hits the pin will elevate the necessary height again. Thus two corrections to the naïve model act in opposite directions, a complication often found in real life. Even perfect agreement of experimental data and theoretical model would not “prove” the latter.
Our pendulums are 1.5 m long, with a 1 cm diameter lead spherical bob and a 3 or 6 mm diameter brass blocking pin. With this setup we can determine and reproduce measurements of hc to within less than a centimeter. The results (Figure 3) can be described by a best fit of hc = (1.96 ± 0.01)hp − 1.1 cm. Our students certainly noted that their results lay systematically below their expectations. But without exception they thought they must have done something wrong. This lack of confidence in their own data indicates the need for their teachers to explain clearly the role of idealized models in a real world.
The experiment was successful in demonstrating for students the tension between inductive and deductive reasoning. They could be made aware of the psychological pressures to “get the right result”, and the way in which this pressure can influence their measurements. We also discussed the conventional nature of the usual lab report, which mirrors the hypothetico-deductive based format of scientific presentations in the physical sciences: theory and expectations first, data en discussions last. This is also the format usually recommended for a lab report. But, as the students have just seen, this is not necessarily the actual sequence of events; hypotheses may be suggested by or even adjusted to experimental data. An experiment from the history of physics is an especially apt device to do show our students the myriad ways in which science and scientists proceed.
